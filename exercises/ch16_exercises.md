# Chapter 16 exercises

## Writing and publishing computational criminology

- Take the abstract of a methods-heavy paper, your own or a published one, and rewrite it so that the question and the finding occupy the first two sentences and the machinery follows. Keep it under 200 words.
- Coding task. Take one figure from your own coursework and produce two versions of it: one designed to flatter the result, with truncated axes or selective windows, and one designed to inform. Annotate the differences and what each does to a reader’s conclusion.
- Draft a data availability statement for a study using police records you are not permitted to share. State what will be deposited, what is restricted and why, and how another researcher could verify your findings despite the restriction.
- Exchange a piece of written-up analysis with a peer and referee it: write a structured review covering the match of method to question, the reproducibility of the methods section, the honesty of the figures, and the claims the design can and cannot support.

---

Coding tasks above can be started from the matching chapter script (`code/python/ch16_*.py` or `code/r/ch16_*.R`) and the synthetic data in `data/synthetic/`. Where an exercise asks for real data, the data directory README lists open sources you can substitute.
