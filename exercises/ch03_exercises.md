# Chapter 3 exercises

## Theory, measurement, and the problem of crime data

- For one offence type, obtain both the police-recorded series and the victimisation-survey series for England and Wales (or the equivalent pair in your jurisdiction) covering at least ten years. Plot the two series together and write 400 words explaining the divergences using the recording pipeline described in this chapter.
- Map the pipeline for one offence in your own jurisdiction: every decision point between an event occurring and a record appearing in a published dataset. Identify the actors at each point and the directions in which their incentives push.
- Sellin argued in 1931 that the value of crime statistics falls as the distance from the offence itself increases, so that police figures are better than court figures and court figures better than prison figures. Test his claim against a modern data source by comparing counts of one offence at two stages of the system.
- Take any predictive model of crime you have read about. Substitute the phrase “recorded by police” for the word “crime” everywhere in its description, and write a paragraph on what the model is now seen to predict and for whom that distinction matters.

---

Coding tasks above can be started from the matching chapter script (`code/python/ch03_*.py` or `code/r/ch03_*.R`) and the synthetic data in `data/synthetic/`. Where an exercise asks for real data, the data directory README lists open sources you can substitute.
